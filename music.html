05 — Music

Home studio songs,
one guy, all the instruments.

Before tech took over my life, music got there first. I started on piano at six, picked up guitar in my early teens, trained as a classical baritone, sang opera at Teatro Colón, then moved through R&B and rock bands before life, work, and college got in the way. Years later I came back to it, turned my home office into a little studio, and started recording songs on Tascam porta studios — guitars, bass, keys, vocals, drums, all layered one track at a time.

Diazepam bio & intro

I grew up in Buenos Aires, Argentina and moved definitively to the US in 1999. I played piano since I was 6 and in my early teens guitar. I never took a single class of either instrument. However, at 15 I started singing classical as a baritone. I sang classical music and studied opera at the Teatro Colón in Argentina. At the age of 22 I quit singing opera, but I was already singing in R&B bands for a few years. That became the perfect opportunity to get more involved with bands, and I started singing in Rock & Roll bands until the age of 25.

But with college studies and work I couldn’t keep up with rehearsals and stopped playing music for many years. A couple of years later I decided to come back and transformed my home office into a studio/rehearsal paradise.

I played all the instruments and everything here was recorded on Tascam porta studios. I can also play these songs live with a simple playback track where I perform the guitar and vocal leads. That gives me the perfect setup to play any time I want without the headaches of other people’s schedules. Yes, it would be nice to have a real band, but for now this gets me going — and I hope you enjoy it too.

Gear: Strat and 6-string acoustic Fender, backup Epiphone Les Paul, Yamaha RBX170 bass, Boss pedals, Dunlop Hendrix wah, Ernie Ball volume pedals, Tascam DP-004 and DP-008, Yamaha YDP, microKorg, Fender Twin 65, Peavey 112 Bass, Alesis SR-16, EV Cobalt mic, Phonic 410 PA, Digitech Vocalist 2, Line 6 BackTrack / Micro Spider / XDS95.

01Riff Raff

This was one of the first songs I recorded — a simple rock & roll track with an AC/DC-style intro. It was also one of my first real experiments with the Boss Loop Station (RC-2) and the Line 6 BackTrack working together, layering guitars while the drum beat and second guitar kept repeating.

The bass lines and synthesizer were recorded with the microKorg in-line, and the lead guitar was recorded from air into a Tascam DP-004. Primitive setup, but it taught me how to build a full arrangement out of loops and overdubs.

02Dreamer

I created this song right after getting my Strat in 2009. Those clean single-coil pickups practically forced these chords out of me. The progression has a floating, wave-like feel, with a soft melody that comes and goes, a jazzy clean-guitar solo in the first part, then a more emotional distorted solo later on.

At the end, the lead guitar drops into shifted-down octaves to create that unison dual guitar/bass sound. The inspiration was Tommy Bolin’s “Dreamer” — and the funny part is we both played the exact same guitar.

03Simon

This one started as a joke. I bought a Strat for my son and, while testing it, came up with these riffs as a simple baseline to practice. I ended up using it so often as a warm-up that I decided it deserved a full song. Then came the microKorg, more guitars, and the DP-004.

This could easily be a Satriani-style backing track. I really enjoy how flexible the soloing feels on this one — every time I play it, I change something. And yes, the harmonizer is absolutely being abused here, but that’s half the fun.

04Hello

I borrowed the base arpeggios from a YouTube guitar version of Evanescence’s “Hello,” then modified the chords enough that it became my own version. Around May 2009 I was still using the microKorg for bass lines and the RC-2 for drums and overdubbing, but this song pushed the setup to its limit.

Hello ended up with around 12 tracks, which means I had to bounce tracks repeatedly on the tiny DP-004. That was around the time I realized I needed a real bass and more recording space.

05Piccadilly is a Circus

The voice at the beginning is my 5-year-old, and for some reason I decided that was the right moment to add lyrics and sing a few lines. So be it. I started working on this after coming back from London, where I had a chance to see my great old friend Jorge “George” C. — an incredible guitarist and a huge inspiration from our Buenos Aires rock-band days in the 90s.

This song is dedicated to him. I believe it was one of the last songs where I used the RC-2, with two different loops switching back and forth. From here on, I stopped using the microKorg as bass and moved to a real Fender Precision and the Yamaha bass setup.

06Bigg's Song (In Memory of Candy Junkie)

This was a hard one. I wrote it in memory of troubled teens, using lyrics adapted from the blog/forum posts of a South Florida teenager who died from a lethal drug overdose in front of a live webcam audience after blogging about his plan.

Selected adapted lines used in the song:

“What hurts the most was being so close / And having so much to say / And watchin’ you walk away...”

“It’s hard to deal with the pain of losing you everywhere I go...”

“I’m not afraid to cry / Every once in a while...”

I cut and reshaped some phrases to make them fit the music, but the point was the same: grief, regret, and the weight people carry when no one sees it. The original forum thread was on Bodybuilding.com.

07Buenos Aires

After coming back from Buenos Aires in April 2010, I started working on this song. By this point the bass was a real Fender Precision, and the drums were programmed on the Alesis instead of coming from the RC-2. I used a lot of harmonizer and layered guitars across this 8+ minute piece.

From the first notes to the final recording, I probably spent at least four months on it. It blends two main melodies: one distorted and one riding above it with harmonizer. I was trying to capture a gray city — all concrete and buildings, streets going dim after 5 p.m. — but still with the charm and density that makes Buenos Aires feel like its own kind of European city.

The guitars and Alesis were recorded in stereo directly into the DP-008. And finally, I found a few places where I could justify using the wah-wah.

08Amanda

After months of work, I finally finished this one. “Amanda” is, of course, Boston’s song from Third Stage (1986), but this is my own version. I used a 6-string acoustic with flanger and chorus to compensate for not owning Tom Scholz’s kind of 12-string texture, played Yamaha bass directly into the DP-008, skipped keyboards entirely, and let the lead guitar handle the harmonization duties.

My original version used the Digitech Vocalist 2 to create the chorus, and it actually worked pretty well live. But when I recorded the voice, it sounded awful. I sent the demo to my friend Jorge “George,” and he told me to kill the effect and just overdub the vocals properly — so I did.
